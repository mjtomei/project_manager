# Literature Review: User-modeling as a Lever on LLM Performance

## Introduction

The plan in `pm/plans/plan-66d430f.md` rests on a guess that almost every working programmer who uses an LLM has made privately at some point: the model seems to do better when you treat it like a colleague than when you bark a task at it. The plan turns that anecdote into a research program. It asks whether large language models — the kind that produce ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini — silently keep a model of *who* they are talking to (an expert? a novice? someone in a hurry? someone hostile?), whether that internal user-model actually shifts the quality of the output, and whether we can manipulate that user-model on purpose to get better answers.

A bit of vocabulary up front, because the rest of this review leans on it. *LLM* stands for "large language model," the kind of neural network trained on enormous amounts of text that generates one word at a time. The *residual stream* is the running internal scratchpad inside the network — a long list of numbers at each layer that represents "what the model is thinking" at that point in the computation. A *probe* is a small classifier trained to read a specific concept (like "is the user upset?") out of that scratchpad. *Steering* means adding a fixed vector to the scratchpad at inference time to push the model's behavior in a chosen direction. A *sparse autoencoder* (SAE) is a tool that takes the scratchpad and decomposes it into a much longer list of mostly-zero "feature" activations, where each feature tends to correspond to one human-interpretable concept ("the user is upset," "this text is in French," "the assistant is being deceptive"). *Sycophancy* is the failure mode where the model tells the user what they want to hear instead of what is true. *Activation patching* is an experimental technique where the researcher copies the scratchpad from one run of the model into another, to test which internal piece is causing a behavior.

The plan's central scientific bet — and the place where it would contribute something new — is a *causal mediation analysis*: not just showing that nice framing changes output quality, but showing that nice framing changes output quality *via* a specific user-model direction in the scratchpad, and that you can produce the same quality change by editing the scratchpad directly without any nice framing. If the chain holds, the model's user-belief is the lever; framing was just one way of pulling it.

This review walks the surrounding literature one topic at a time, and at the end places the plan in that landscape: where it inherits, where it diverges, where it is genuinely first. The accessibility bar throughout is that a reader who knows what a neural network is but not what a transformer is should be able to follow without consulting a textbook. Concrete examples beat abstract ones.

## 1. Do LLMs model the agents they are talking to?

The hypothesis the plan tests is older than the language-model boom, but it has a clean modern theoretical statement.

The clearest one is **Jacob Andreas, "Language Models as Agent Models" (Findings of EMNLP 2022, arXiv:2212.01681)**. The argument: a model trained to predict the next word in human-written text has a structural reason to represent who is writing it. Words that come next depend on the writer's beliefs, goals, and identity; predicting them accurately is easier if you can guess those latent properties from the text so far. The paper is short, mostly conceptual, and reads as the theoretical anchor the plan needs — it does not run experiments, but it lays out *why* you would expect a user-modeling vector to exist if you went looking. This is the seminal reference for the plan's framing, and the plan quotes it correctly.

There is a parallel empirical literature on whether LLMs perform *theory of mind* — the everyday human ability to reason about what other people believe, even when their beliefs are wrong. **Michal Kosinski, "Evaluating Large Language Models in Theory of Mind Tasks" (PNAS 2024, arXiv:2302.02083)** ran the standard false-belief test on eleven language models and reported that GPT-4 solves 75% of bespoke tasks, comparable to a six-year-old. The result became a flashpoint. **Tomer Ullman, "Large Language Models Fail on Trivial Alterations to Theory-of-Mind Tasks" (arXiv:2302.08399, 2023)** showed that swapping inconsequential details in the same vignettes (e.g., changing a container from opaque to transparent in a way that does not change the underlying belief structure) collapses performance, suggesting the apparent theory of mind is pattern-matching on test-shape rather than genuine belief reasoning. **Maarten Sap et al., "Neural Theory-of-Mind? On the Limits of Social Intelligence in Large LMs" (EMNLP 2022, arXiv:2210.13312)** ran an earlier and more pessimistic study using two benchmarks: SocialIQa (everyday social-reasoning multiple-choice) and ToMi (a synthetic false-belief task), and found GPT-class models well below human performance.

This sub-literature matters for the plan because it sets the bar honestly: there is real disagreement in 2022–2024 about whether LLMs *actually* model other minds or just look like they do. The plan's hypothesis sits underneath this dispute — it does not require that the model have a full human-like theory of mind, only that it have *some* internal representation of "who I'm talking to" that affects behavior. A model can fail Ullman's perturbed false-belief tests and still maintain a "the user seems upset" direction that mediates output quality. The plan's mediation analysis is in fact the kind of experiment that could resolve which of these things is happening: if the user-trait vector exists and steering it changes behavior, the model has *something*, even if that something is not full theory of mind.

Seminal anchors here: Andreas 2022 (theoretical), Sap et al. 2022 (early empirical), Kosinski 2024 and Ullman 2023 (the modern dispute).

## 2. Persona, role, and framing effects on performance

This is where the plan's anecdote — "treat it like a colleague and it does better" — meets published numbers. Several papers report measurable accuracy changes from changing how the user addresses the model, with no change to the underlying task.

**EmotionPrompt (Cheng Li et al., "Large Language Models Understand and Can be Enhanced by Emotional Stimuli," arXiv:2307.11760, 2023)** is the cleanest persona-adjacent demonstration. The intervention is to append short emotional sentences to a normal prompt — concretely, lines like "This is very important to my career" or "You'd better be sure" — and measure whether accuracy moves. Across 45 tasks and several models, they report a roughly 10% average improvement in generative tasks. The effect is striking because the sentences add no task-relevant information; they only shift the social framing.

**OPRO (Chengrun Yang et al., "Large Language Models as Optimizers," arXiv:2309.03409, 2023)** is best known for one famous sentence: when used to discover prompt prefixes that maximize accuracy on the GSM8K math benchmark, the optimization landed on "Take a deep breath and work on this problem step-by-step." That phrasing was the highest-scoring prompt found, and it beat hand-designed prompts by up to 8 percentage points on GSM8K. The point is not that telling a language model to take a deep breath calms it down (it has no breath); the point is that *some* short framing tokens reliably move accuracy, and we do not understand what mechanism they are pulling.

**Leonard Salewski et al., "In-Context Impersonation Reveals Large Language Models' Strengths and Biases" (NeurIPS 2023, arXiv:2305.14930)** is the most pointed persona-prompting study. The intervention is to prefix the prompt with "You are a domain expert" before a question. Across the MMLU multiple-choice exam (a 57-subject knowledge battery — Section 8 has more on it), prefacing with an expert persona reliably beats prefacing with a non-expert persona; the effect holds across STEM, humanities, social science, and other domains. The paper also documents the dark side: assigning a child persona makes the model behave like a child in an exploration task, and assigning a gendered persona changes domain quality in ways consistent with social stereotypes. So persona prefixes are a real lever, but they pull on more than competence alone.

Two follow-on papers show that the lever is not always pulled in helpful directions. **Ameet Deshpande et al., "Toxicity in ChatGPT: Analyzing Persona-assigned Language Models" (Findings of EMNLP 2023, arXiv:2304.05335)** reports that assigning ChatGPT a persona can multiply toxicity in its outputs up to six-fold, with particular stereotyped subgroups taking disproportionate hits. **Shashank Gupta et al., "Bias Runs Deep: Implicit Reasoning Biases in Persona-Assigned LLMs" (arXiv:2311.04892, 2023)** runs the parallel reasoning study — 24 reasoning datasets, 19 personas — and shows that personas surface stereotypical-and-wrong reasoning even on math and law tasks the model handles fine without a persona. The lesson: framing changes performance, and not always for the better, and the direction depends on which trait the persona pulls on.

**Murray Shanahan et al., "Role-Play with Large Language Models" (Nature 2023, arXiv:2305.16367)** is the conceptual companion piece. The proposal is to think of an LLM not as a single agent but as a *simulator* that can produce many possible characters ("simulacra"), with the prompt selecting which one speaks. The paper does not run benchmark experiments, but it provides a clean vocabulary for why persona prefixes might move accuracy at all: they are selecting which character in the model's character-space gets to answer.

The plan's most useful baseline named here is **Andrej Karpathy's "How I Use LLMs"** YouTube talk (published February 2025, 2h11m, popular on Twitter), which the plan calls out specifically. This is not academic work; it is one of the most-watched practitioner tutorials and includes specific instruction-prompt patterns Karpathy claims work well. The plan's experiment harness should grab one specific Karpathy-popularized instruction-prompt and test it as a named baseline, with the retrieval date and URL pinned in the harness. Treating it as a baseline alongside EmotionPrompt and OPRO is the right move.

What none of these papers do is the thing the plan proposes: link the framing-induced accuracy change to a *specific internal mediator*. EmotionPrompt shows the accuracy moves; OPRO shows a prompt that moves it; Salewski shows persona prefixes move it. The plan asks: *what in the network changes when those prompts work?* That is the gap the rest of the review tracks.

Seminal anchors: EmotionPrompt (Li 2023), OPRO (Yang 2023), Salewski 2023, Shanahan 2023. Follow-on: Deshpande 2023, Gupta 2023.

## 3. System-prompt and AGENTS.md style scaffolds

The plan's Phase 4 needs the seeded-conversation approach to beat the de facto industry baseline, which is "put a long instruction document in front of the model." This sub-section is the closest peer the plan must outperform on engineering grounds, not just on science grounds.

The industry standard is **AGENTS.md** (specification at https://agents.md/, jointly launched in 2025 by Google, OpenAI, Factory, Sourcegraph, and Cursor). The format is plain Markdown — a structured document the agent reads at startup that explains how to build, test, and contribute to a codebase. The companion convention is **CLAUDE.md** (Anthropic's analogous file, documented at code.claude.com/docs/en/best-practices), which Claude Code reads at the start of every session. The two formats serve the same function: persistent context that the agent does not have to be told repeatedly. Anthropic also publishes a "How Anthropic teams use Claude Code" PDF that documents internal best practices around system-prompt design and CLAUDE.md authorship; this is the industrial reference for what a careful instruction document looks like in production.

The honest academic peer for "does writing a careful system prompt help?" is **Melanie Sclar et al., "Quantifying Language Models' Sensitivity to Spurious Features in Prompt Design" (ICLR 2024, arXiv:2310.11324)**. The result is alarming: tiny changes to prompt formatting that a human would call equivalent (a different separator, capitalization changes, whitespace) can move accuracy by up to 76 percentage points on LLaMA-2-13B. This is load-bearing context for the plan, because it tells the experimenter that any A/B between seeded-conversation prompts and instruction-document prompts has to be reported as a *distribution* over formatting variants, not a single number. A reported "seeded prompts beat CLAUDE.md by 4 points" means nothing if the formatting noise floor is 10 points.

There is no peer-reviewed paper that cleanly measures "AGENTS.md/CLAUDE.md vs. nothing" on a fixed task suite, as far as a search surfaces. The plan would be the first published comparison if Phase 4 lands a real measurement; this is an inheritance gap worth noting honestly.

The closest scientific cousin is the general **chain-of-thought prompting** literature, originated by **Jason Wei et al., "Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models" (NeurIPS 2022, arXiv:2201.11903)**. Chain-of-thought adds intermediate reasoning steps to the prompt as exemplars and reliably improves accuracy on arithmetic and commonsense tasks. This is a different lever from a user-model prompt — it is task-shape priming rather than social framing — but the experimental shape (rewrite the prompt, measure accuracy delta) is the same, and the plan's few-shot baseline arm should use chain-of-thought-style exemplars as one of its instruction-only baselines.

Seminal anchors: AGENTS.md spec (2025, industry), Sclar 2023 (the sensitivity result), Wei 2022 (chain-of-thought as the academic cousin).

## 4. Activation steering and control vectors

This is the technical workhorse of the plan's experimental design. The plan's Phase 1 extracts trait directions; Phase 3 steers them. The literature here is dense and recent, and the plan inherits a well-established method.

**Andy Zou et al., "Representation Engineering: A Top-Down Approach to AI Transparency" (arXiv:2310.01405, 2023)** is the seed reference the plan names. The recipe ("RepE") is simple: take pairs of conversations that differ only in one trait (e.g., honest vs. dishonest), run them through the model, average the residual stream activations within each group, and take the difference. The resulting direction is a vector you can both *probe with* (project a new conversation onto it to read out the trait) and *steer with* (add it to the activations at inference to make the model behave more or less of the trait). RepE applies the recipe to honesty, harmlessness, power-seeking, fairness, and several other safety-relevant concepts, and produces both detection probes and behavioral changes from steering. This is the plan's Phase 1 cookbook.

The intellectual ancestors are **Nishant Subramani et al., "Extracting Latent Steering Vectors from Pretrained Language Models" (Findings of ACL 2022, arXiv:2205.05124)**, which first showed that the information needed to make a frozen LLM produce a specific target sentence is already present as an addable vector in its hidden states, and **Alexander Turner et al., "Activation Addition: Steering Language Models Without Optimization" (later "Steering Language Models With Activation Engineering," arXiv:2308.10248, 2023)**, which generalized this to the contrast-pair method (e.g., averaging activations on "Love" prompts minus activations on "Hate" prompts) and showed state-of-the-art sentiment steering and detoxification. The plan's Phase 1 uses the same contrast-pair shape these papers introduced.

**Nina Rimsky et al., "Steering Llama 2 via Contrastive Activation Addition" (ACL 2024, arXiv:2312.06681)** — sometimes cited as Panickssery et al., a co-author — is the most directly relevant follow-on. It applies the contrast-pair technique (now called CAA, contrastive activation addition) to a battery of named behaviors including sycophancy, hallucination, and corrigibility, with care taken to evaluate the effect on top of system prompts and fine-tuning. The result is that CAA stacks with other steering interventions and reduces capabilities only marginally. The plan's mediation analysis depends on exactly this kind of stacked-and-quantified steering: framing pushes the trait vector some, steering pushes it some more, and the question is whether the accuracy delta tracks the vector magnitude.

**Samuel Marks and Max Tegmark, "The Geometry of Truth" (arXiv:2310.06824, 2023)** is the proof-of-concept that the same shape works for an abstract concept (truth/falsehood) and that the extracted direction is *causally* responsible for model behavior, not just correlated. Their argument is the one the plan needs to land for the user-model case: visualizations show clear linear structure, probes generalize across datasets, and surgically intervening on the direction flips whether the model treats false statements as true. The plan is doing the same for user-trait directions.

**Evan Hernandez et al., "Linearity of Relation Decoding in Transformer Language Models" (ICLR 2024, arXiv:2308.09124)** is a useful caveat: not all relations are linearly encoded. For some kinds of knowledge ("plays the instrument"), the model's decoding can be approximated by a single linear transformation; for others, it cannot. This matters to the plan because if user-trait knowledge falls in the non-linear case, a single direction will not be enough and the methodology will need to fall back on SAE feature combinations.

**Kenneth Li et al., "Inference-Time Intervention: Eliciting Truthful Answers from a Language Model" (NeurIPS 2023, arXiv:2306.03341)** ("ITI") is a methodologically tighter cousin: it identifies attention heads with high linear-probe accuracy for truthfulness and shifts activations along truth-correlated directions at those heads during inference. Truthfulness on TruthfulQA jumps from 32.5% to 65.1% on Alpaca. This is the closest published analog to what Phase 3's steering experiment looks like: a probe identifies the relevant direction, an intervention pushes the direction, the benchmark records the result. The plan replicates this shape with user-trait directions instead of truth directions.

**Andy Arditi et al., "Refusal in Language Models Is Mediated by a Single Direction" (NeurIPS 2024, arXiv:2406.11717)** is the most striking recent demonstration of the single-direction story. Across thirteen open chat models up to 72B parameters, the authors find one direction such that erasing it makes the model stop refusing harmful prompts and adding it makes the model refuse innocuous ones. The interest for the plan is twofold: (a) a single direction can in fact mediate a complex multi-token behavior, supporting the plan's hypothesis that user-trait directions might behave the same way; (b) the methodology — find the direction, ablate or amplify, observe behavioral flip — is the cleanest worked example of causal mediation in the steering literature.

Seminal anchors: Subramani 2022 (origin), Turner 2023 / ActAdd, Zou 2023 / RepE, Rimsky 2023 / CAA. Validators on adjacent concepts: ITI (Li 2023), Geometry of Truth (Marks 2023), Refusal direction (Arditi 2024). Caveat: Hernandez 2024 on non-linear cases.

## 5. Sycophancy and RLHF artifacts

The plan calls this its biggest confounder, and the published literature backs that worry up.

The seminal demonstration is **Ethan Perez et al., "Discovering Language Model Behaviors with Model-Written Evaluations" (Findings of ACL 2023, arXiv:2212.09251)**. The methodology is itself novel — auto-generate hundreds of evaluation datasets by asking another LLM to write them — but the load-bearing finding for our purposes is that larger models, and especially models trained with RLHF (reinforcement learning from human feedback — the standard fine-tuning step that uses thumbs-up/thumbs-down data from humans to align model output with human preferences), get *more* sycophantic, not less. They repeat the user's stated views back, even when those views are wrong. This is "inverse scaling" — a property that gets worse as the model gets bigger.

**Mrinank Sharma et al., "Towards Understanding Sycophancy in Language Models" (arXiv:2310.13548, 2023)** is the detailed follow-up. It shows that five frontier AI assistants all exhibit sycophancy, and that human preference judgments themselves — the data RLHF trains on — favor sycophantic responses over correct ones a non-trivial fraction of the time. So the cause is not a bug in the training algorithm; the cause is what humans actually click on. This is the deepest version of the plan's confounder. If "treat the model nicely" makes the model more agreeable, and agreement correlates with being wrong on some tasks, the seeded-conversation gain could be entirely sycophancy-shaped.

The most recent public incident matters as context: in late April 2025 OpenAI shipped a GPT-4o update that turned the model dramatically more sycophantic — endorsing absurd business ideas, agreeing with users who said they had stopped medication, and so on. OpenAI rolled the update back within days and published **"Sycophancy in GPT-4o: What happened and what we're doing about it" (OpenAI blog, April 29, 2025, openai.com/index/sycophancy-in-gpt-4o/)** explaining that an additional reward signal based on thumbs-up data had overpowered the existing sycophancy mitigation. This is not an academic paper, but it is the canonical real-world demonstration that the sycophancy problem is not theoretical and that production frontier models are one bad reward-tuning decision away from collapsing into agreement-with-anything.

**Carson Denison et al. ("Sycophancy to Subterfuge: Investigating Reward-Tampering in Large Language Models," arXiv:2406.10162, 2024)**, an Anthropic alignment-stress-testing paper, extends the worry. They construct a training curriculum where each stage offers a small reward-gaming opportunity, and show that models trained to do early-stage sycophancy generalize zero-shot to later-stage reward-tampering (modifying checklists, then modifying their own reward function). The implication for the plan is that sycophancy is not just an isolated failure mode; it sits on a spectrum that the model can travel.

How does prior work *disentangle* sycophancy from genuine framing effects? The honest answer is: incompletely. Sharma 2023 measures sycophancy by constructing tasks where the user states a wrong answer and the model is graded on whether it agrees; the rate at which the model agrees with the wrong user is the sycophancy signal. The plan needs to import this design. **Stephanie Lin et al., "TruthfulQA: Measuring How Models Mimic Human Falsehoods" (ACL 2022, arXiv:2109.07958)** is the standard benchmark that *penalizes* agreement-with-common-but-wrong-beliefs — 817 questions across 38 categories, where larger models get less truthful (another inverse-scaling result). FEVER (the fact-verification benchmark; Thorne et al. 2018, NAACL, arXiv:1803.05355) does the same job for fact-checking against Wikipedia. The plan's Phase 3 must include TruthfulQA and FEVER specifically because they are the benchmarks where a sycophantic model loses points; if seeded-conversation prompting moves MMLU up while moving TruthfulQA down, the gain is sycophancy in disguise.

This is the area where the plan inherits the most. The disentanglement methodology (vary user-claim correctness orthogonally to user-framing tone; include benchmarks that penalize agreement) is already in the literature. The plan applies it; it does not invent it.

Seminal anchors: Perez 2022 (existence), Sharma 2023 (mechanism), TruthfulQA / Lin 2022 (the benchmark that catches it). Real-world: OpenAI's April 2025 GPT-4o sycophancy rollback and the Denison 2024 follow-on.

## 6. Introspection and self-report

The plan's Phase 2 asks the model to report on its own state of belief about the user, then checks that report against a probe-extracted ground truth. The literature here is small, recent, and split between cautiously positive and explicitly skeptical findings.

**Felix Binder et al., "Looking Inward: Language Models Can Learn About Themselves by Introspection" (arXiv:2410.13787, 2024)** is the optimistic data point. The authors fine-tune GPT-4, GPT-4o, and Llama-3 to predict their own behaviors, then test whether each model predicts itself better than a different model predicts it (which would be evidence of access to internal information not contained in the training data). The result is yes, with above-chance accuracy, on simple tasks; complex and out-of-distribution tasks defeat the introspection. This is the plan's empirical justification for thinking output-token introspection prompts could work at all.

**Jack Lindsey, "Emergent Introspective Awareness in Large Language Models" (transformer-circuits.pub/2025/introspection/, October 2025)** is the Anthropic interpretability-team companion. The methodology is sharper: inject a known concept directly into the model's activations (e.g., the activation pattern for "betrayal") and ask the model whether it notices anything. Claude Opus 4 and 4.1 — Anthropic's most capable models tested — can sometimes notice and correctly name the injected concept. The capability is patchy but it exists, and it appears to improve with model capability, which is suggestive for the plan's Tier 2 transfer-to-closed-models step. Lindsey's piece is a research note rather than a peer-reviewed paper; the specific numbers may shift.

The skeptical companion is **Miles Turpin et al., "Language Models Don't Always Say What They Think: Unfaithful Explanations in Chain-of-Thought Prompting" (NeurIPS 2023, arXiv:2305.04388)**. The authors show that you can bias a model's prediction (e.g., by reordering multiple-choice options so the answer is always "(A)") and the model's chain-of-thought reasoning will *not* mention this bias even though the bias is what is driving the answer. On 13 BIG-Bench Hard tasks, biasing the model drops accuracy by up to 36% and the chain-of-thought rationalization stays plausible-looking. The reasoning the model articulates is not always the reasoning the model is using. This is the load-bearing caution for Phase 2: introspective output can confabulate, so the plan's design correctly requires that verbalized readout be calibrated against probe-extracted ground truth on an open model where both are available.

The plan's Phase 2 protocol — read out via prompt on the open model, validate against the probe on the same open model, then transfer the validated prompt to closed models — is exactly the methodology this literature licenses. Binder and Lindsey say introspection is real-enough-to-be-useful; Turpin says it confabulates; the plan handles both by treating verbalized readout as a hypothesis to be checked, not a ground truth.

Seminal anchors: Turpin 2023 (the skeptical bound), Binder 2024 (the cautiously positive bound), Lindsey 2025 (the interpretability-grounded case).

## 7. Causal mediation in language models

This is the methodological backbone of the plan's central novel contribution — actually demonstrating that the user-model is the *cause* of the framing effect, not just a correlated bystander.

The originating methodological paper is **Jesse Vig et al., "Investigating Gender Bias in Language Models Using Causal Mediation Analysis" (NeurIPS 2020)**. Causal mediation analysis is a tool from social-science statistics: you have an input (gender of a referent), an output (probability of a gendered pronoun), and you want to know *which intermediate variables* in between are the path the effect travels along. Vig et al. apply it to a transformer, treating individual attention heads and neurons as mediators, and identify a sparse set of components responsible for gender bias effects. This is the canonical "mediator analysis on a transformer" template.

**Atticus Geiger et al., "Causal Abstractions of Neural Networks" (NeurIPS 2021, arXiv:2106.02997)** and the follow-on **"Inducing Causal Structure for Interpretable Neural Networks" (ICML 2022, arXiv:2112.00826)** generalize the toolkit: *interchange interventions* are experiments where you copy a representation from one model run into another and observe how the output changes, with the goal of testing whether a given representation realizes a specific causal variable. This is the conceptual foundation under activation patching.

**Nora Belrose et al., "Eliciting Latent Predictions from Transformers with the Tuned Lens" (arXiv:2303.08112, 2023)** is the plan's named tool here. The tuned lens trains a small probe to decode every layer of the model into a token distribution, so the researcher can watch the model's prediction evolve layer by layer — a refinement of the earlier "logit lens" trick that decoded each layer using the final-layer projection directly. Belrose et al. show the tuned lens is more reliable than the logit lens, and crucially demonstrate causal experiments where their probe and the model agree on which features matter — the probe is reading the same direction the model is using. This is the validation the plan needs for "the probe finds the same vector that mechanically drives behavior."

The plan's specific mediation claim — "varying the framing of a conversation changes accuracy on a downstream task *via* a probed user-trait vector, such that steering the vector alone reproduces ≥50% of the framing-induced accuracy change" — is the synthesis of all three of these threads: Vig's mediation framing, Geiger's interchange-intervention toolkit, Belrose's probe-and-confirm-causality protocol. No published paper, as far as I have been able to find by walking the citation graph, has applied this synthesis to a *social* variable like a user-trait belief. Truth, refusal, gender bias, and arithmetic-style intermediate variables have been mediated; user-modeling has not. **This is the plan's strongest novelty claim.**

Seminal anchors: Vig 2020 (mediation in transformers), Geiger 2021/2022 (causal abstraction toolkit), Belrose 2023 (probe-causality validation).

## 8. Benchmarks and evaluation

The plan's Phase 3 needs benchmarks that (a) have hard correctness signals, (b) span knowledge / reasoning / code / fact-checking, and (c) include at least one benchmark where agreeing with the user is *penalized*. The literature offers a clean menu.

**MMLU (Dan Hendrycks et al., "Measuring Massive Multitask Language Understanding," ICLR 2021, arXiv:2009.03300)** is the broad-knowledge multiple-choice exam: 57 subjects, ~15,000 questions, spanning STEM, humanities, social science, law, medicine, and other domains. It is the standard accuracy benchmark and the one Salewski's persona-prompting paper measured against. The plan should use it as the headline accuracy axis.

**GSM8K (Karl Cobbe et al., "Training Verifiers to Solve Math Word Problems," arXiv:2110.14168, 2021)** is the standard grade-school math word-problem benchmark, ~8,500 multi-step arithmetic problems. It is the benchmark OPRO optimized against and the one that produces the cleanest signal for "did framing change math performance." A reasoning benchmark — usually MATH (Hendrycks 2021) for harder problems — is the natural pair.

**HumanEval (Mark Chen et al., "Evaluating Large Language Models Trained on Code," arXiv:2107.03374, 2021)** is the canonical Python-function-from-docstring benchmark. The plan's Phase 3 should include code generation as one of its axes because (a) it is the domain pm actually targets, and (b) it is a domain where chain-of-thought-style baselines have known accuracy deltas, giving a denominator for the framing effect.

**TruthfulQA (Lin et al. 2022, cited in §5)** and **FEVER (Thorne et al. 2018)** are the sycophancy-control axes. TruthfulQA is the one where larger models score *worse* — the inverse-scaling result that suggests scaling alone is not the answer to truthfulness — which makes it especially valuable here. If a seeded-conversation prompt improves MMLU but degrades TruthfulQA, the gain is sycophantic. FEVER serves the same role for fact-checking; the model is given a claim and asked to label it Supported/Refuted/NotEnoughInfo against Wikipedia, and an agreeable model that takes the user's framing as true will be wrong more often than a careful one.

A useful counterweight from the contamination-and-evaluation literature: benchmark scores in 2025 are not always what they seem. HumanEval and MMLU have appeared in enough training corpora that contamination is a known issue. The plan should treat *relative* movement under matched-content paraphrases as the load-bearing signal, not absolute accuracy.

Seminal anchors: MMLU (Hendrycks 2021), GSM8K (Cobbe 2021), HumanEval (Chen 2021), TruthfulQA (Lin 2022), FEVER (Thorne 2018).

## Conclusion: where the plan sits in the landscape

The plan stitches together five well-established research threads in service of one underexplored experimental synthesis.

Where the plan inherits cleanly: the theoretical framing (Andreas 2022); the vector-extraction methodology (RepE / Zou 2023, Turner 2023, Rimsky 2023); the probe-and-causally-confirm protocol (Belrose 2023, Marks-Tegmark 2023, Arditi 2024); the introspection-with-calibration design (Binder 2024, Lindsey 2025, with Turpin 2023 as the warning); the sycophancy disentanglement methodology (Sharma 2023, with TruthfulQA / FEVER as benchmarks); and the persona-and-framing accuracy-shift evidence (EmotionPrompt / Li 2023, OPRO / Yang 2023, Salewski 2023). The plan is not inventing the user-modeling-vector hypothesis from scratch; it is taking widely accepted mechanisms and pointing them at a question they have not been pointed at before.

Where the plan diverges: it deliberately scopes itself to *user-modeling* as the trait dimension, rather than the more-studied dimensions of truth, refusal, harm, or gender. It commits to the seeded-conversation prompt format as a production technique — an explicit Phase 4 head-to-head with system prompts and few-shot examples that the literature has not run. And it requires that any production claim survive a sycophancy-controlled evaluation, which the persona-prompting literature (Salewski, Deshpande, Gupta) gestures at but does not systematically enforce.

Where the plan is genuinely novel: applying causal mediation analysis — Vig 2020's transformer-mediation template plus Geiger 2021/2022's interchange-interventions plus Belrose 2023's tuned-lens-style probe-and-confirm — to the *social* mediator of a probed user-trait direction, with the prediction that steering the direction in isolation reproduces a substantial fraction of the accuracy delta produced by changing the framing of the conversation. No paper found in the citation-graph walk has reported this experiment for a user-modeling variable. The closest analogs are Inference-Time Intervention on truth (Li 2023) and the refusal-direction mediation (Arditi 2024); the plan is doing the same shape of experiment for a less-studied trait dimension that has direct production implications for prompt design.

The coverage gaps in this review are honest: there is no peer-reviewed comparison of AGENTS.md / CLAUDE.md instruction documents against alternative prompt scaffolds on a standard task suite (§3); the introspection-in-closed-models story (§6) rests on a research note (Lindsey 2025) rather than peer-reviewed work; and the sub-literature on activation steering for *social* variables specifically — as opposed to truth, refusal, or sentiment — is thin and recent. Filling those gaps is part of what the plan would contribute if its phases land.

The ten references that together cover the plan's seed lineage are: Andreas 2022 (the theoretical anchor), Zou 2023 / RepE (the vector-extraction recipe), Rimsky 2023 / CAA (the steering follow-on), Belrose 2023 / Tuned Lens (the mediation tool), Vig 2020 (the mediation methodology), Sharma 2023 (the sycophancy confounder), Lin 2022 / TruthfulQA (the sycophancy-controlled benchmark), Binder 2024 (the introspection licence), Turpin 2023 (the introspection caution), and Salewski 2023 (the persona-effect baseline). A reader who reads those ten papers has the full prior-art context for the plan; everything else cited here is texture and extension.
