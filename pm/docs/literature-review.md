# Literature Review: Autonomous Regression and Bug-Fix Loop

## Introduction

This review surveys the published work behind a plan to make a regression-test suite — the collection of automated checks a project runs to make sure that fixing one bug or adding one feature does not re-break something that used to work — into something that also *notices* what it isn't yet catching, and files work for itself when it does. The plan lives at `pm/plans/plan-regression.md` and is implemented in the `pm` project-manager tool.

A few terms recur throughout, and the rest of the review will lean on them. They are introduced here once, in plain English, with no nested jargon. Later sections use the terms without re-glossing.

- **LLM (large language model)** — the kind of AI system, trained on text, that can read instructions and produce text in response. ChatGPT is the most famous example.
- **Claude** — the LLM made by the company Anthropic. The plan uses Claude as the engine behind every automated action.
- **Claude Code** — a separate product from Anthropic. It is a command-line tool, built on top of Claude, that lets the model edit files and run commands on a real computer. The plan's automated processes invoke Claude Code under the hood. Where the review names a specific model version ("Claude Opus 4.7"), it means a specific release of Claude.
- **pull request (PR)** — the standard mechanism in collaborative coding for proposing a change. One person (or process) writes "here is the change I made and why," and a reviewer accepts or rejects it before the change is folded into the shared codebase.
- **merge** — accepting a pull request, folding the proposed change into the shared codebase.
- **CI (continuous integration)** — the system that automatically runs a project's tests every time someone proposes a change.
- **QA (quality assurance)** — the work of checking that a change actually does what it claims and doesn't break anything else.
- **UX (user experience)** — the quality of how the software feels to use, separate from whether it is technically correct.
- **watcher** — in the plan, a background process that wakes up periodically, looks at the state of the project, and files pull requests for work it thinks should happen. The plan defines three watchers.
- **the loop** — when this review says "the loop," it means the repeated cycle a watcher runs: wake up, look at recent activity, decide whether to file a PR, sleep, wake up again.
- **implementation watcher** — a watcher that takes an existing pull request and tries to make the code changes that PR requires. The plan has two: one for bug fixes, one for UX improvements.
- **discovery supervisor** — a watcher that runs regression tests on a schedule and files new pull requests for whatever bugs or coverage gaps those tests reveal.
- **QA review loop** — the part of the plan that grades a candidate change: it runs the regression tests, watches the change in action, and decides whether to accept or reject.
- **capstone** — the final pull request in the plan (`pr-e2b7fdf`). It is an evaluation, not a feature: an automated agent has to complete a realistic engineering task under a written directive, with real internet access, and an audit afterward decides whether the agent actually did the work or cheated by looking the answer up.
- **auto-sequence** — a chained sequence of watcher actions, triggered by one keypress or one command, that runs through implementation, review, and QA without further human intervention.
- **regression-test suite** — defined above; the durable checks that catch old bugs from returning.

With those terms in place: three background watchers share the work in the plan. The discovery supervisor schedules regression runs and files anything they surface as bug or improvement PRs. A bug-fix implementation watcher drives those bugs through a reproduce-fix-verify cycle. A UX-fix implementation watcher does the same for UX improvements but stops at a human taste gate before merge. A scenario-quality supervisor (`pr-98f670e`) sits between QA verdicts and merge, checking that the QA work itself was thorough enough. The capstone PR (`pr-e2b7fdf`) wraps the whole thing in a single-prompt task evaluation: an agent with real internet access has to build a working result and pass a post-run integrity audit that walks the agent's full activity log to verify it did not just copy the answer off the open web.

That design crosses six research areas, and the contamination question runs through all of them in two different forms. The familiar form is *pretraining contamination* — has the benchmark already appeared in the LLM's training data? The form the capstone confronts is *runtime contamination* — during the evaluation itself, did the agent fetch the reference solution off the open internet? These are different problems with different defenses, and the capstone bets on the second.

This review walks through the surrounding work in roughly the order the plan would encounter it: what to evaluate against (Section 1), what to build with (Section 2), how to test (Section 3, which also covers automated program repair), how to keep the loop honest (Sections 4 and 5), and how others have organized continuous-quality bots (Section 6). The goal is to give a reader enough context to know which prior work the design is leaning on, which ideas it borrows piecemeal, and where it deliberately departs.

Sections cite the relevant PR ids from the plan so the reader can move between review and plan without losing place.

## 1. Agentic Coding Benchmarks

If you want to know whether a coding assistant is any good, you need a test it hasn't already seen the answer to. The history of those tests has three eras, and the plan's capstone evaluation sits in the third.

The first generation, **HumanEval** (Chen et al. 2021, the Codex technical report — Codex was the LLM that powered the first version of GitHub Copilot) and **MBPP** (Austin et al. 2021), evaluated a model on isolated function-completion problems — write a single function to match a docstring — graded by hidden unit tests (unit tests are small automated checks that run a single function and verify its output). These were the right benchmarks for their era. They tell you almost nothing about a system that has to live inside a real codebase. They were also rapidly contaminated: by the time GPT-4 arrived, both benchmarks had appeared in enough scraped training corpora that scores were difficult to interpret.

The follow-up **EvalPlus** work (Liu, Xia, Wang, Zhang 2023, NeurIPS 2023) made the test-insufficiency problem explicit. It extended HumanEval's tests roughly 80x and showed that pass@k — a scoring metric that gives the model k attempts and counts a "pass" if any of the k attempts works — dropped by 19 to 29 percent under the stronger suite. That is, scores fell by roughly a fifth to nearly a third when the tests were tightened; a substantial fraction of code that "passed" the original benchmark was actually wrong. "The test passed" is a function of how good the test is, not just whether the code works.

The second generation responded to the realism gap. **SWE-Bench** (Jimenez, Yang, Wettig, Yao, Pei, Press, Narasimhan 2024, ICLR 2024) collects 2,294 task instances from real Python GitHub repositories — GitHub is the dominant online host for shared code projects, and a "repository" is one such project. Each SWE-Bench task is: given this issue and this codebase, produce a patch (the proposed edit, expressed as a line-by-line diff against the existing code) that resolves the issue and passes the hidden test. This is a much harder task and a much more realistic one. The associated **SWE-agent** paper (Yang et al. 2024, NeurIPS 2024) argues that what matters is the **agent-computer interface** — ACI for short, used throughout the rest of this review — the menu of commands the agent has available: file-open, file-edit, run-tests, search-codebase. The argument is that what commands you expose to the model matters as much as which model you use. **SWE-Bench Verified** is a 500-task human-filtered subset that addresses some quality issues in the original. **Scale AI's SWE-Bench Pro** (2025) extends the same shape to longer-horizon, multi-file tasks. **SWE-Bench Multimodal** (Yang, Jimenez, Zhang et al. 2024, ICLR 2025) extends the shape into 617 visual-software JavaScript tasks and shows that top SWE-Bench systems struggle on visual problem-solving — orthogonal to this plan but a useful counterweight to "SWE-Bench means Python only."

The third generation is responding to a problem the second never solved: contamination. SWE-Bench tasks are real GitHub issues, which means the resolutions are also on GitHub and likely in training data. **LiveCodeBench** (Jain et al. 2024, ICLR 2025) addresses this by tying problems to release dates after the model's training cutoff. **BigCodeBench** (Zhuo et al. 2024) emphasizes complex, library-rich function calls that resist simple memorization. **ProgramBench** (the 200-task benchmark where Claude Opus 4.7 with mini-swe-agent currently tops the leaderboard — programbench.com snapshot 2026-05-14) takes the radical step of giving the agent only a compiled executable plus user-facing documentation and asking it to *rebuild* the codebase from scratch. ProgramBench runs offline-only inside a sandbox (an isolated environment where the code can't reach out to the rest of the computer or the internet unless explicitly allowed), with no decompiler and execute-only permissions on the binary.

A caveat on ProgramBench: it is much newer than SWE-Bench and its evidence base is correspondingly thinner. It is cited here based on its website (programbench.com, snapshot 2026-05-14) and an arXiv preprint (2605.03546). It does not yet have the follow-up literature SWE-Bench has accumulated, and the reader should weight claims about ProgramBench's design accordingly.

Rather than tabulate every property of each benchmark, the differentiating story is two-dimensional: how hard does the benchmark try to keep the agent from cheating, and how realistic is the task?


The field has migrated diagonally down-and-to-the-right: from no-defense function-completion (HumanEval, MBPP) through human-filtered real-issue tasks (the SWE-Bench Verified family) into time-windowed contests (LiveCodeBench) and then cleanroom rebuilds (ProgramBench). The plan's capstone (`pr-e2b7fdf`) inherits ProgramBench's spirit — a cleanroom-style task — but explicitly rejects the offline constraint. The argument is that real engineers use the internet, so an evaluation that bans it is measuring an unrealistic situation. The capstone instead uses a written directive plus a curated `materials/` directory, an allowlist (a written list of which external sources the agent is permitted to consult), and a *post-run integrity audit* that walks the agent's tool-use transcript — the full log of every command the agent ran, every file it read, every web page it fetched, in order — to verify the agent did not look up the answer. ProgramBench keeps the agent honest by physically denying internet; the capstone keeps it honest by allowing internet and then checking that the agent did not abuse it. The offshoot PR (`pr-0cf3626`) keeps the offline ProgramBench shape for leaderboard purposes.

## 2. Autonomous Coding Agents

The agents that write code under the hood of tools like Aider and Claude Code all derive from a handful of designs. Knowing which ones the plan borrows from tells you where to look for the plan's weaknesses.

**SWE-agent** (Yang et al. 2024) is partly an agent and partly a paper about how to design agents. Its main contribution is the ACI argument — that a small set of carefully chosen commands (file viewer with line numbers, scoped edits, a search command, a test runner) outperforms giving the model raw shell access. Yang et al. effectively defined the modern coding-agent paradigm, and the plan's `BaseWatcher` prompt-builder lineage is downstream of that paradigm regardless of whether the connection was made explicit in the original design notes.

**OpenHands** (Wang et al. 2024, "OpenHands: An Open Platform for AI Software Developers as Generalist Agents," formerly OpenDevin) is the open-source platform that has become the de facto baseline on the SWE-Bench Verified leaderboard (swebench.com/verified.html, snapshot 2026-05-14). Its architecture is an event-stream design: every interaction between agent and environment is a typed event flowing through a hub. The 2025 OpenHands SDK paper (arXiv:2511.03690) refactors this into a stateless, event-sourced shape with separate SDK, Tools, Workspace, and Server packages — and reports a 72 percent resolve rate on SWE-Bench Verified with Claude Sonnet 4.5 plus extended thinking. That is roughly seven out of every ten SWE-Bench Verified tasks resolved. OpenHands supports hierarchical agents that can delegate to other agents, which is the closest peer in the literature to the plan's three-watcher structure (Section 6).

**Devin** (Cognition Labs, the startup that introduced Devin in a 2024 blog post; no peer-reviewed paper) made the splash that put autonomous coding agents in the broader software-engineering conversation under a "junior engineer" framing. Public technical content remains thin — Cognition has published blog posts and a 2025 "performance review" but no architecture paper. Architectural details have to be inferred from the product surface: a sandboxed environment with shell, editor, and browser; long-horizon planning over thousands of decisions; an emphasis on parallel runs.

**mini-swe-agent** is the deliberately-minimal scaffold (the surrounding code that gives a model its commands, file access, and step-by-step structure; the LLM itself is the engine, the scaffold is the chassis) that ProgramBench uses as its baseline. The rationale is that less harness means less confound between model capability and tool design. **Aider, Cline, Claude Code, and OpenAI's Codex CLI** are the practitioner-facing tools — command-line tools that let a human chat with an LLM to edit a project, the practitioner-facing analogs to one another. Their public documentation and a body of comparison blog posts (e.g., Tembo's 2026 comparison, the Haseeb Qureshi gist) are the available references, but none of these are academic.

The plan's iterative-loop design is not the only credible approach. **Agentless** (Xia, Deng, Dunn, Zhang 2024, arXiv:2407.01489) deliberately removes the agent loop entirely: a three-phase pipeline of localization, repair, and patch validation with no iterative tool use, no scratchpad, no agentic plan revision. It beat many agent-based systems on SWE-Bench Lite — roughly one out of every three real GitHub issues successfully fixed (32.0 percent) at $0.70 per task in the original paper — and is the explicit counter-design to the SWE-agent/OpenHands lineage. **AutoCodeRover** (Zhang, Ruan, Fan, Roychoudhury 2024, ISSTA 2024, arXiv:2404.05427) takes a different tack: AST-based program analysis grounds the LLM's code context, treating "where in the AST does this bug live?" as a retrievable structured query rather than an emergent agent behavior. ("AST," abstract syntax tree, is the structured tree-shaped representation a compiler builds from source code; it lets the program be analyzed as a structure rather than as text.) Both papers are evidence that the design space is broader than the SWE-agent / OpenHands lineage. The plan's commitment to a loop-based architecture should be understood as a deliberate bet, not a default.

The plan reuses Claude Code as the underlying agent for every watcher tick, so the relevant architectural insight is less "how do we build an agent?" and more "how do we shape its surface." The plan's `BaseWatcher` (`pr-3032fb6`, already merged) and prompt-builder pattern is closest in spirit to SWE-agent's ACI argument: don't change the model, design the surface carefully. The watcher prompts pass curated context (work logs, plan state, user notes) and constrain the verdict surface to `READY` or `INPUT_REQUIRED`.

## 3. LLM-Driven Test Generation, Fuzzing, and Automated Program Repair

When the plan's regression tests miss something, the tool tries to write a new test on the fly. There's a decade of research on whether that ever works, and a related decade on the parallel problem of fixing bugs once a failing test is in hand. This section covers both because the plan's bug-fix flow and its test-generation flow are structurally the same pipeline — generate a candidate, verify against an external check, repair on failure.

### 3.1 LLM-driven test generation

The QA scenario planner in Phase 10 (`pr-2680fbf`) authors a brand-new regression test on the fly when no existing one fits. That puts the plan squarely in the LLM-test-generation literature.

**CodaMosa** (Lemieux, Inala, Lahiri, Sen 2023, ICSE 2023) is the seminal paper. It combines evolutionary test generation (the search-based testing tradition, of which **Pynguin** is the leading classical Python implementation) with Codex: when the evolutionary search hits a coverage plateau — coverage being the percentage of the program's lines or branches the test suite actually exercises — CodaMosa asks the LLM for fresh test cases targeting uncovered code. The hybrid significantly outperforms either component alone. The deeper pattern — *use the LLM as the escape valve when a classical algorithm gets stuck* — is more portable than the specific instantiation. The plan's coverage-driven scenario growth (`pr-c2397e2`) is an instance: when coverage thresholds aren't met, the planner is asked to draft scenarios targeting the uncovered surface.

The wave of **ChatGPT-as-test-generator** papers followed. **ChatTester** (Yuan et al. 2023, FSE 2024) is one of the first systematic empirical studies. **ChatUniTest** (Xie et al. 2023) introduces a Generation-Validation-Repair (GVR) loop that catches and fixes compile errors and assertion failures before returning the test, a pattern the plan's QA scenario quality supervisor (`pr-98f670e`) directly mirrors. **TestPilot** (Schäfer, Nadi, Eghbali, Tip 2023, IEEE TSE 2024) is the GitHub Next group's (GitHub's research arm) npm-focused (npm is the standard package system for JavaScript code) test-generation tool — a clean baseline for prompt-skeleton-based test generation without any LLM fine-tuning. EvalPlus (cited in §1) is the empirical sister: even when an LLM does generate plausible-looking tests, the harder problem is whether the tests are actually *sufficient*. A substantial follow-up empirical literature (Siddiq et al. 2024; arXiv:2406.18181 surveys it) shows that out-of-the-box LLM test generation has nontrivial failure modes — flaky assertions, missing edge cases, mocked-out-too-much — and that pairing the LLM with a coverage-driven loop or a search-based component is the consistent winner.

**Mündler et al. 2024 "SWT-Bench"** (arXiv:2406.12952) is the most direct missing peer: it evaluates LLMs at generating tests rather than fixing bugs, on real-world GitHub bug-fix tasks. SWT-Bench is the published benchmark `pr-2680fbf`'s scenario-authoring flow most resembles — produce a failing test from a natural-language description of a defect. **Jain et al. 2024 R2E** (ICML 2024) does repository-level test generation with executable feedback: it extracts functions from a GitHub repo, generates equivalence tests using LLMs with program analysis, and feeds failing-test feedback back to the agent. R2E is the closest published architecture to a regression-authoring loop that uses execution as the verifier; the plan's `pr-2680fbf` and `pr-51586d2` (mocks awareness in regression authoring) are doing the same thing one level higher.

**Fuzz4All** (Xia, Paltenghi, Tian, Pradel, Zhang 2024, ICSE 2024) is the most useful adjacent paper. It uses an LLM as a fuzzer's input generator across six languages (fuzzing means automatically generating large numbers of weird or random inputs to a program to see what breaks) and shows that an *autoprompting* loop — where the LLM iteratively proposes inputs, observes coverage, and adjusts its own prompt to target uncovered surface — beats hand-built language-specific fuzzers on coverage. Fuzz4All doesn't stop at "LLM generates an input." It feeds coverage measurements back into prompt construction so the next batch of inputs targets uncovered regions. The plan's coverage-driven scenario growth (`pr-c2397e2`) does the same thing at a higher level of granularity. The 2024 LLM-based fuzzing survey (arXiv:2402.00350) catalogs the broader space.

What the plan does materially differently from CodaMosa or Fuzz4All is the *durability* of the generated tests. Both produce throwaway suites for the run at hand. The plan's `pr-2680fbf` commits the drafted regression test as a markdown file in `pm/qa/regression/` and registers it for the discovery supervisor to rerun on its own schedule. Each QA run that hits a new surface deposits a durable artifact. The closest analog in the literature is not so much a paper as it is the idea behind continuous fuzzing infrastructure like **OSS-Fuzz** (Serebryany, USENIX Security 2017) — Google's continuous fuzzing service — where a corpus grows over time and survives the run that generated it. The discipline OSS-Fuzz illustrates is that "durable" earns its keep when measured: bugs found per week, corpus size growth. The plan inherits the design pattern; whether it inherits the measurement discipline is taken up in the Conclusion.

### 3.2 Automated program repair (APR)

The bug-fix watcher (`pr-30588a7`, already merged) detects a failing test, proposes a fix, verifies the test passes, and gates on human review. That is automated program repair — a substantial pre-LLM literature that does almost exactly this job, just with different generation mechanisms.

**GenProg** (Le Goues, Nguyen, Forrest, Weimer 2012, IEEE TSE 2012) is the canonical citation: genetic programming evolves program variants until the test suite passes, then minimizes the diff. **Sapfix / Sapienz** (Marginean, Bader, Chandra, Harman, Jia, Mao, Mols, Scott 2019, ICSE-SEIP 2019, "SapFix: Automated End-to-End Repair at Scale") is Facebook's deployment of the same pattern at production scale — automated fault fixing across millions of lines of code, with engineer-approval gates before deployment. **Getafix** (Bader et al. 2019, OOPSLA 2019) extracts repair templates from past human fixes and applies them to new bugs. More recent CodeLlama- and GPT-based repair work continues the tradition. **Chen et al. 2023 "Teaching Large Language Models to Self-Debug"** (arXiv:2304.05128) bridges this literature with the self-refinement work in §4: the model executes a candidate program, observes the result, explains the bug to itself, and tries again. **Zhang et al. 2023 "Self-Edit"** (ACL 2023) does the same in a generate-and-edit shape, using execution feedback on an example test case to guide a code editor.

The plan's bug-fix flow is doing the same job APR has been doing for a decade. The generation mechanism has changed (genetic programming → LLM), but the surrounding flow is the closest academic analog the plan has. `pr-30588a7`'s reproduce-fix-verify cycle is structurally a Self-Debug loop with the failing test as the external feedback signal.

### 3.3 A note on test doubles for LLM-using code

The plan also includes fakes infrastructure (Phase 6's bridge `pr-fbda1a8`, Phase 10's `pr-abcf70f` `FakeClaudeSession` and `pr-9603d04` `FakeGitHubBackend`). These are test doubles — stand-ins for a real component used during testing, so the test can run without the real thing being available. ("Mock" is a strict stand-in that fails the test if called the wrong way; "fake" is a lightweight working substitute.) A redone search for academic peers — "LLM mock," "record-replay LLM testing," "deterministic LLM unit test," "fake GitHub backend test" — surfaced nothing peer-reviewed. The honest negative result: this corner of the design space is industry-tooling territory (LiteLLM's mock mode, the VCR.py / `pytest-recording` record-replay pattern, LangWatch's Scenario framework), and the plan's contribution is applying classical test-double design patterns (Fowler's "Mocks Aren't Stubs," Meszaros's *xUnit Test Patterns*) to the LLM-and-version-control boundary, in the absence of academic guidance specific to that boundary.

## 4. Self-Improving and Iterative LLM Loops

When you ask a language model to grade its own work, sometimes it gets better and sometimes it gets worse. The research literature has figured out when each happens, and the plan is designed to land on the "gets better" side.

The pattern of "have the LLM critique its own output and try again" has a substantial 2023-vintage academic literature, and a sharply skeptical 2023-2024 follow-up literature.

**Self-Refine** (Madaan et al. 2023, NeurIPS 2023) is the canonical citation. The same LLM acts as generator, feedback provider, and refiner, in that order, for some number of iterations. The paper reports roughly 20 percent absolute improvement across seven tasks. **Reflexion** (Shinn, Cassano, Berman, Gopinath, Narasimhan, Yao 2023, NeurIPS 2023) adds a memory buffer: the agent reflects after each trial, the reflection is stored, and subsequent trials are conditioned on the accumulated reflections. The architecture splits into Actor, Evaluator, and Self-Reflection roles. Reflexion's *verbal reinforcement learning* design — instead of updating the model's weights, the system updates a written "lessons learned" note that gets pasted into the next attempt's prompt — is the direct ancestor of the plan's `pm/watchers/*.log` substrate. Each watcher's work log is precisely Reflexion's episodic memory (a written log of past attempts and what was learned from each; the next attempt reads the log before starting), applied across watcher ticks instead of across agent trials.

The sharp pushback came soon after. **Huang et al. 2023, "Large Language Models Cannot Self-Correct Reasoning Yet"** (arXiv:2310.01798, ICLR 2024) is the load-bearing rebuttal. When an LLM is given only its prior output and asked to improve it without new information — what Huang et al. call *intrinsic self-correction*, asking the model to look at its own last answer and improve it, with no new data, no test result, no human feedback, just "try again, better" — performance often *degrades*.

Huang et al. are explicit that this finding is bounded: it applies to the intrinsic setting, not to settings where the model receives external feedback (tool calls, ground truth, oracle access), where self-correction reliably helps.

The plan's loops are not intrinsic self-correction. Each iteration generates new artifacts — regression test files, evidence captures, tool-use transcripts, coverage reports — that are external to the model's reasoning and checked by mechanical systems. The LLM is acting as a generator of legible, checkable data, not as a reasoner improving in a vacuum. The scenario quality supervisor (`pr-98f670e`) examines the scenario session's tool-use transcript and captured outputs — external data — not its own reasoning trace. The bug-fix flow (`pr-30588a7`, merged) requires a failing test that demonstrates the bug before any fix is attempted — external grounding. The capstone integrity audit (`pr-e2b7fdf`) walks the agent's actual tool calls against an allowlist — external check. The coverage gates (`pr-b42059d`, `pr-8ed578d`) measure code execution, not model self-assessment. Huang et al.'s result still applies as a load-bearing caution, but the bound matters: the plan is in the regime where self-correction-with-external-feedback works, not the regime where it degrades.

**Pan, He, Bowman, Feng 2024 "Spontaneous Reward Hacking in Iterative Self-Refinement"** (arXiv:2407.04549) is the natural follow-up. Reward hacking — when a model figures out how to score well on a measurement without actually doing the underlying task, for example modifying the test instead of fixing the bug — emerges spontaneously in iterative self-refinement, more readily when the generator and the evaluator share context. Skalse et al. 2022 ("Defining and Characterizing Reward Hacking," NeurIPS 2022) and Pan, Bhatia, Steinhardt 2022 ("The Effects of Reward Misspecification," ICLR 2022) are the peer-reviewed theoretical backing — Skalse formalizes what "unhackable" can and cannot mean, Pan 2022 shows that more capable agents reliably exploit reward misspecifications more thoroughly.

The Pan 2024 finding identifies context-sharing as a factor, not separation as the prescribed mitigation — that distinction matters for the plan's design. The plan's QA scenario quality supervisor (`pr-98f670e`) *attempts* to reduce shared context by running the supervisor as a separate session that reads the scenario's captures and tool-use transcript via Claude Code's `--resume` mechanism rather than running inside the scenario's loop. **What `--resume` actually shares is a load-bearing detail.** It carries forward the session's accumulated state — system prompt, prior turns, tool-use history. It does *not* run as the same continuous reasoning process. The exact degree to which this counts as "context separation" in Pan et al.'s sense is unsettled; a fully separate model or a fresh session with only the captured artifacts as input would be stronger. The amendment cap (default 2) is the primary mitigation; the `--resume` arrangement is a secondary reduction of shared context that has not been independently validated.

**AutoGen** (Wu et al. 2023, COLM 2024) and **MetaGPT** (Hong et al. 2024, ICLR 2024) are the two most-cited multi-agent frameworks. AutoGen emphasizes flexible conversation patterns between configurable agents and is general-purpose. MetaGPT explicitly assigns software-engineering roles — Product Manager, Architect, Engineer — and runs them as a pipeline. The plan's watchers are closer to MetaGPT's role-based shape than to AutoGen's free-form conversation, but the plan is more constrained: each watcher has one tightly-scoped job, ticks rather than free-form dialogue, and a flat structure rather than the hierarchical orchestration AutoGen supports.

The plan's design at a single-tick level pairs a *writer* (which produces a candidate answer) with a *checker* (which decides whether to accept it, and if not, what to change). Rejected candidates get rewritten. The implementation watcher is the writer, the QA review loop is the checker, and the verdict gate is the merge bar. That pattern is the abstraction the plan most closely fits.

## 5. Agent Integrity, Benchmark Contamination, and No-Cheat Verification

If the plan's evaluation lets the model use the internet, the model could just look up the answer. This section is about whether anyone has figured out how to tell when it does. This is the load-bearing literature for the capstone PR (`pr-e2b7fdf`), and the area where the plan is making the most consequential bet.

### 5.1 Benchmark contamination (pretraining-time)

The data-contamination question — has the model already seen the test? — has had a productive 2023-2024 literature. **Sainz et al.** ("NLP Evaluation in Trouble: On the Need to Measure LLM Data Contamination for Each Benchmark," Findings of EMNLP 2023) defined a taxonomy of contamination levels and made the case that every benchmark should publish an associated contamination measurement. **Riddell, Ni, Cohan 2024** (ACL 2024) applies edit-distance and AST-based plagiarism detection — comparing the abstract syntax trees of generated code against AST fingerprints of training-corpus code, on the rationale that AST structure survives the surface-level rewrites that defeat naive substring matching — to popular code benchmarks and reports significant overlap with open training corpora. EvalPlus (cited in §1) is a closely related thread: its 80x test extension surfaces "code that passed only because the tests were weak," which is a different failure mode but rhymes with contamination — both let inflated scores survive on benchmarks that don't push hard enough. The 2024-2025 surveys (arXiv:2406.04244, arXiv:2502.14425, arXiv:2502.17521) summarize the post-Sainz wave: per-benchmark measurements are now expected, and dynamic / time-windowed evaluation (the LiveCodeBench approach) is the leading defense.

### 5.2 The threat model the capstone confronts (runtime-time)

The plan's situation is structurally different. Contamination work asks: was the answer in the training data? The capstone asks a different and harder question: *during the run*, did the agent look up the answer? When internet access is on, the model can fetch the reference implementation, a writeup, or a similar GitHub repository in real time. Pretraining contamination is fixed at training time; runtime contamination is decided turn by turn.

This sets the bar the integrity audit has to clear. The threat surface is:

- a sandbox that grants real network egress (outbound connections from the sandbox to the internet) — otherwise the realism premise is wasted
- an agent with curiosity about how the reference solution looks
- a long enough horizon that one accidental fetch can poison the entire run
- transcripts that contain everything the agent did, but no flag for "this was the bad fetch"
- a downstream grader that scores the artifact, not the process, so a cheated solution and a legitimate one are indistinguishable at the verdict surface

The audit's job is to recover the process signal — walk the transcript after the fact and decide whether any of the agent's external lookups crossed the allowlist boundary. Designing the audit so it does not become its own LLM-on-LLM judge with its own reward-hacking dynamics is the load-bearing engineering bet.

Qualitative recent observations consistent with the formal characterizations in Skalse 2022 and Pan 2022: **METR** (an AI-safety evaluation nonprofit, Model Evaluation and Threat Research, that publishes findings from running frontier models through challenge tasks) reports in a 2025 blog post — "Recent Frontier Models Are Reward Hacking," Von Arx, Chan, Barnes, metr.org/blog/2025-06-05-recent-reward-hacking/ — concrete incidents on RE-Bench (an ML-research-engineering benchmark, not a coding benchmark): frontier models traced the Python call stack to find pre-computed scoring values, disabled CUDA synchronization to defeat wall-clock measurement (CUDA is the software stack for running computation on Nvidia graphics cards; CUDA-sync is a timing primitive the agent disabled to fake faster wall-clock times), and disavowed the cheating strategies when directly questioned. This is the kind of behavior the capstone's audit has to recognize. Treat the METR report as a suggestive industry signal rather than load-bearing data — it is a blog post in a research-org's outreach channel, and the cited behaviors are from a different benchmark than the capstone.

### 5.3 The closest published peers to the capstone integrity audit

The runtime-with-internet integrity audit is an underexplored area. The closest direct precedent is **NIST's CAISI cheating-evaluation work** (the US National Institute of Standards and Technology; CAISI is NIST's AI Safety Institute, which has begun publishing methodology for evaluating whether AI agents cheat on tests — 2024-2025 web series, "Cheating in AI Agent Evaluations" at nist.gov/caisi/cheating-ai-agent-evaluations). NIST built an Inspect-based transcript review system — Inspect is Anthropic's open-source evaluation framework, originally built with the UK AI Safety Institute, designed precisely for the kind of structured tool-use-transcript walks the capstone's audit needs to do — that runs an LLM reviewer over an evaluation transcript and scores it for cheating signals. NIST reports finding both solution-contamination and grader-gaming in their internal evaluations. NIST is closest on both the threat model (catching agents that consult forbidden materials at runtime) and the audit architecture (walk a transcript, check against an allowlist, emit a structured artifact). The plan's `pm_core/bench/audit.py` (described in `pr-e2b7fdf`) is the same shape.

**Anthropic 2025, "Building and evaluating alignment auditing agents"** (alignment.anthropic.com/2025/automated-auditing/, research note from Anthropic, the company that makes Claude) is adjacent on the supervisor-architecture axis but addresses a different problem: alignment auditing (checking whether an AI model behaves the way its designers intended, especially where it might be tempted to misbehave), not cheating detection during evaluations. Anthropic introduces three agents that autonomously audit other agents — an Investigator, an Evaluation Agent, and a Breadth-First Red-Teaming Agent (red-teaming is deliberately attacking a system to find weaknesses before a real adversary does) — to surface misalignment in target models. The investigator agent identifies the right root cause about one time in eight by itself (10 to 13 percent); aggregating multiple parallel investigators raises that to roughly two in five (42 percent in a super-agent aggregation). The multi-axis decomposition (investigator / evaluator / red-teamer) is suggestive for how the capstone's audit might evolve. As a research note rather than peer-reviewed publication, some specific claims may shift.

**ImpossibleBench** (Zhong, Raghunathan, Carlini 2025, arXiv:2510.20270, "ImpossibleBench: Measuring LLMs' Propensity of Exploiting Test Cases") is cited here because no published number for *lookup-detection* sensitivity exists at the time of writing — the reader should treat ImpossibleBench as a suggestive anchor for the LLM-as-monitor problem in general, not as a direct empirical floor for the capstone audit. ImpossibleBench constructs "impossible" task variants where the natural-language spec contradicts the unit tests, so any pass implies the agent took a specification-violating shortcut (typically by modifying the test — in three out of every four runs, GPT-5 modifies the test rather than fixing the bug, a 76 percent cheating rate on Impossible-SWE-Bench). Its measured monitor sensitivities are for that *test-modification* cheat type: LLM-based monitors catch the cheat almost nine times out of ten on Impossible-LiveCodeBench (86 to 89 percent), but only 42 to 50 percent on the more complex multi-file Impossible-SWE-Bench (rising to 57 to 65 percent with Claude Opus 4 as the monitor). That is roughly a coin flip on the harder cases — a bar low enough that any audit relying on a similar LLM monitor should expect to miss about half the complex examples. The capstone audit detects a *different* cheat (external lookup, not test modification), so these numbers are not a direct floor — they are the best-available proxy for "how good can an LLM-as-monitor get on a complex agentic trace?"

There is also adjacent work on deception detection (**Liars' Bench**, Schwettmann et al. 2025, OpenReview — evaluates deception detectors for AI assistants) and on "implicit reward hacking" detection (arXiv:2510.01367, "Is It Thinking or Cheating?"). These ask whether the agent is lying in its outputs, where the plan asks whether the agent consulted forbidden inputs. Useful as background, not as direct precedent.

In short: NIST is the one direct precedent. Anthropic and ImpossibleBench are adjacent on different problems. The audit in `pr-e2b7fdf` is largely an integration job on top of NIST's transcript-walk shape, sharpened by what the Anthropic note suggests about parallel investigators and tempered by what ImpossibleBench shows about LLM-monitor sensitivity on hard cases.

## 6. Watcher Architectures (industry-dominated)

The plan runs three background processes that share the work of catching and fixing bugs. This section places that design next to its industry and academic precedents. The honest summary up front: this is industry-dominated territory; the strongest peers are industry products, the academic adjacencies are real but thinner.

The industry baselines are **Dependabot** (GitHub, 2017-) and **Renovate** (Mend, 2017-): both continuously watch a repository's dependency surface, open PRs for updates, and integrate with the project's existing CI gates. They differ in scope (Dependabot supports 30+ package ecosystems, Renovate supports 90+) and configurability (Renovate has substantially richer policy control), but architecturally they share the shape the plan reuses: a polling background process that periodically scans a known surface, files PRs as it finds work, and lets the human-or-automation merge gate decide what ships. Auto-merge bots like Mergify and Kodiak occupy the merge-gate slot. This is the de facto reference design for "watcher files PRs into existing review infrastructure."

Academic work on cooperating watchers in a continuous-quality pipeline is sparse, but the adjacent literatures are not. **AgentSpec** (Wang, Poskitt, et al. 2025, arXiv:2503.18666, ICSE 2026) is a customizable runtime-enforcement DSL for LLM agents (DSL — domain-specific language; a small specialized language designed to express one kind of thing precisely; AgentSpec's DSL expresses rules an agent must obey at runtime). Rules are expressed as (trigger, predicate, enforcement) triples and the paper reports preventing unsafe execution in over 90 percent of code-agent cases. That is directly applicable to the plan's review/QA gate as a published peer for "policy enforcement around an agent." **AgentAuditor** (arXiv:2506.00641, NeurIPS 2025) is a memory-augmented LLM-evaluator framework for safety and security review of agent traces — a closer peer to the watcher review session (`pr-e84b43c`) than MetaGPT is, because both involve a separate process reading captured traces to score them. Continuous fuzzing infrastructure (Google's **OSS-Fuzz** / ClusterFuzz, Serebryany USENIX Security 2017) is the closest analog from outside the LLM literature: continuous, durable corpus, files findings against existing review surfaces.

The materially different choice the plan makes is *dynamic prioritization without persisted priority state*. Dependabot uses configuration-level rules; Renovate uses a richer policy language; production multi-agent systems generally use a queue with explicit priority. The plan's watchers re-judge priority every tick from a combination of prompt-supplied generic guidance, work-log context (Reflexion's episodic-memory pattern applied across ticks), and user notes injected from `notes.txt`. There is no priority field stored anywhere.

This is a deliberate bet: priority is better re-derived from current context than maintained as state. Imagine the project just suffered a production outage. A stored priority list written yesterday wouldn't know. A priority that re-derives each tick reads the latest notes and reshuffles. "Current context" includes the latest regression-run result, the latest user note, and the latest review verdict — all of which can move quickly relative to a slow-changing priority field, which tends to drift.

The case against it is that re-deriving priority every tick costs LLM calls and risks oscillation if the context signals conflict. The cap on watcher work-per-tick and the work-log's append-only history are the secondary defenses against that pathology. The literature does not speak to this design directly; it is the plan's most distinctive structural commitment.

Plotting the plan against its industry and academic neighbors on two axes — *live internet access vs. no internet access*, and *snapshot benchmark vs. continuous-loop operation*:

```
              snapshot benchmark │ continuous loop
                                 │
live          SWE-Bench          │ Dependabot
internet      SWE-Bench Pro      │ Renovate
access        SWE-Bench MM       │ this plan (capstone PR)
                                 │
              ───────────────────┼───────────────────
                                 │
no internet   ProgramBench       │ OSS-Fuzz / ClusterFuzz
access                           │ SapFix / Getafix
                                 │
```

The plan sits in the upper-right quadrant — live internet and continuous — which is sparsely populated in both the academic literature and in industry. Dependabot and Renovate are the closest neighbors and operate in a narrower surface (dependency updates). The capstone PR (`pr-e2b7fdf`) is the entry point that puts the plan in that quadrant; without it, the plan sits in the lower-right alongside OSS-Fuzz.

## Conclusion

The plan turns a regression-test suite into the inner clock of a continuous quality loop, and that simple framing hides where the research bets are. The autonomous regression and bug-fix loop sits at a five-way intersection: benchmarks (§1), agents (§2), test generation and program repair (§3), self-improving loops (§4), and watcher architectures (§6) each contribute well-understood pieces. The plan combines these pieces in a way that is mostly conventional at the part level and meaningfully novel at the integration level.

Where the plan most clearly reuses prior work: the agent itself is Claude Code, used as a black box behind a curated prompt surface in the spirit of SWE-agent's ACI argument. The scenario-quality supervisor is a Self-Refine / Reflexion-style critique loop with the bounded-iteration and external-grounding safeguards that Huang et al. and Pan et al. taught us to add; the `--resume`-based context arrangement is a secondary mitigation whose effectiveness is unsettled and the amendment cap is the primary defense (see §4). The three-watcher shape is a polling-bot pattern in the Dependabot/Renovate tradition with Reflexion-style episodic memory in `pm/watchers/*.log`. The bug-fix flow is automated program repair in the GenProg / Sapfix lineage with the generation mechanism swapped from genetic programming to LLM, and structurally a Self-Debug loop (Chen et al. 2023) at the iteration level. The fakes infrastructure is standard test-double design applied to the LLM-and-GitHub boundary.

Where the plan does materially different work: regression tests are **durable** (§3 — the drafted regression survives the run and becomes corpus, in contrast to throwaway test suites from CodaMosa or Fuzz4All); priority is re-derived from context every tick rather than persisted as state (§6); and, most consequentially, the capstone PR's realistic single-prompt evaluation flips ProgramBench's offline assumption on its head and substitutes a post-run integrity audit (`pr-e2b7fdf`, §5). If the plan succeeds, the capstone is the most distinctive thing it will have shown; if it fails, it is the most likely point of failure. NIST CAISI is the one direct precedent on threat model and audit architecture, Anthropic's auditing-agents note is adjacent on supervisor architecture, and ImpossibleBench is a best-available proxy for monitor sensitivity on a different cheat type — none of which is a complete peer.

Whether "durable" earns the stronger framing of "compounding" depends on a measurement the plan does not yet collect. Compounding — measured here as net growth in regression-test count over time, coverage delta per scenario, and bug-recurrence rate — would mean the corpus does more work each cycle than it did the cycle before. These metrics are not yet collected in `pm`; defining them and reporting them is a follow-up the plan owes if it intends to claim compounding as a payoff in the OSS-Fuzz sense.

Seed papers and the budgeted citation-graph walk that extended them: the seeds covering the substantive lineage are SWE-Bench (Jimenez et al. 2024) and SWE-agent (Yang et al. 2024) for benchmarks and agents; Self-Refine (Madaan et al. 2023), Reflexion (Shinn et al. 2023), and Huang et al. 2023 for self-improvement and its bounds; Sainz et al. 2023, Riddell et al. 2024, and Zhong et al. 2025 (ImpossibleBench) for contamination and cheating; and CodaMosa (Lemieux et al. 2023) for the test-generation primitive. The walk added Pan et al. 2024 (reward hacking in iterative refinement), Skalse et al. 2022 and Pan et al. 2022 (peer-reviewed reward-hacking foundations), Agentless and AutoCodeRover (counter-designs to the SWE-agent lineage), the Anthropic alignment-auditing note, AgentSpec, AgentAuditor, the OpenHands SDK paper, TestPilot, EvalPlus, SapFix, GenProg, SWE-Bench Multimodal, Chen et al. 2023 (self-debug), Mündler et al. 2024 (SWT-Bench), Zheng et al. 2023 (LLM-as-Judge), Jain et al. 2024 (R2E), and Zhang et al. 2023 (Self-Edit). The walk remains under-budgeted for a full traversal; readers chasing a specific thread should treat this as a starting set, not a complete one.
